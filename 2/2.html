01 — Introduction

Our Cosmic
Neighbourhood

The Solar System formed approximately 4.6 billion years ago from a dense cloud of interstellar gas and dust called a solar nebula. A massive gravitational collapse of this nebula created our Sun at the centre, while the remaining material flattened into a protoplanetary disc from which the planets, moons, asteroids, and comets eventually coalesced.


It is located in the Orion Arm of the Milky Way galaxy, roughly 26,000 light-years from the galactic centre. The Sun's gravity holds everything in the system together, from the innermost rocky worlds to the icy bodies of the far Oort Cloud.

The Star at our Centre

The Sun

Type G Yellow Dwarf Star · Middle of Main Sequence

The Sun is the star at the centre of our Solar System. It is an almost perfect sphere of hot plasma, heated to incandescence by nuclear fusion reactions in its core. The Sun radiates the energy that sustains almost all life on Earth via photosynthesis, and drives Earth's climate and weather. It accounts for about 99.86% of the total mass of the Solar System.

The Sun's core reaches temperatures of about 15 million °C, where hydrogen nuclei fuse to form helium, releasing tremendous amounts of energy. This energy slowly travels outward through radiation and convection zones before reaching the photosphere — the visible surface — at around 5,500 °C.

01

Planet 01 · Innermost

Mercury

Terrestrial Planet · Closest to the Sun

Mercury is the smallest planet in the Solar System and the closest to the Sun. Despite being nearest to the Sun, it is not the hottest planet — that title goes to Venus, due to Mercury's lack of atmosphere. Its surface is covered in craters, ridges, and plains, resembling our own Moon. Its highly elliptical orbit and slow rotation result in extreme temperature swings from –180 °C at night to 430 °C during the day.

Mercury has an enormous iron core making up about 85% of its radius — the largest relative core of any planet. This is thought to be the result of an ancient giant impact that stripped away its outer layers.

Planet 02 · Evening Star

Venus

Terrestrial Planet · Earth's Twin in Size

Venus is the second planet from the Sun and the hottest in our Solar System, with surface temperatures averaging 465 °C — hot enough to melt lead. This extreme heat is caused by a runaway greenhouse effect driven by its thick atmosphere of carbon dioxide, with clouds of sulphuric acid blanketing the entire planet. Atmospheric pressure on its surface is 92 times that of Earth's.

Venus rotates extremely slowly and in the opposite direction to most planets (retrograde rotation), meaning the Sun rises in the west and sets in the east. One Venusian day is longer than one Venusian year. It is often called Earth's "evil twin" due to its similar size but drastically different conditions.

Planet 03 · Our Home

Earth

Terrestrial Planet · The Blue Marble

Earth is the third planet from the Sun and the only known planet in the universe confirmed to harbour life. It has liquid water on its surface — covering 71% of it — and a nitrogen-oxygen atmosphere that shields life from harmful radiation. Earth's active geological and hydrological cycles, along with its position in the Sun's habitable zone, have allowed complex life to thrive for over 3.5 billion years.

Earth's large Moon stabilises its axial tilt, moderating its climate. Its strong magnetic field, generated by a molten iron outer core, deflects solar wind. Earth is the densest planet in the Solar System and the largest of the four terrestrial planets.

Planet 04 · The Red Planet

Mars

Terrestrial Planet · Future Destination

Mars is the fourth planet and the second-smallest in the Solar System. Its reddish appearance is due to iron oxide (rust) covering much of its surface. Mars hosts the tallest volcano in the Solar System — Olympus Mons (21 km high) — and the longest canyon — Valles Marineris (4,000 km long). Evidence of ancient river valleys and lake beds suggests liquid water once flowed on its surface.

Mars has two small moons — Phobos and Deimos — which are likely captured asteroids. Its thin atmosphere is mostly carbon dioxide, offering little protection from solar radiation or extreme cold. It is the most explored planet beyond Earth.

Planet 05 · The Giant

Jupiter

Gas Giant · King of the Planets

Jupiter is the largest planet in the Solar System — so massive that all other planets combined would still only be about 70% of its mass. It is a gas giant composed primarily of hydrogen and helium, with no solid surface. Its most iconic feature, the Great Red Spot, is a colossal storm that has raged continuously for over 350 years and is larger than Earth itself.

Jupiter has the fastest rotation of any planet, completing one revolution in just under 10 hours. Its powerful magnetic field — the strongest of any planet — and at least 95 known moons make it a mini solar system of its own. The four largest moons (Io, Europa, Ganymede, Callisto) are called the Galilean moons.

Planet 06 · The Ringed Wonder

Saturn

Gas Giant · The Jewel of the Solar System

Saturn is the sixth planet from the Sun and is renowned for its spectacular ring system — the most extensive and visible of any planet. The rings are composed of billions of ice and rock particles spanning up to 282,000 km from the planet's centre but only about 10–20 metres thick on average.

Saturn is the least dense planet in the Solar System — so light it would float on water. With at least 146 known moons, it has the largest moon count of any planet. Its largest moon, Titan, has a thick atmosphere and liquid methane lakes — the only known body besides Earth with stable surface liquids.

Planet 07 · The Tilted Giant

Uranus

Ice Giant · The Sideways Planet

Uranus is the seventh planet from the Sun and the third largest by diameter. Unlike all other planets, Uranus rotates on its side — its axial tilt is approximately 98°, meaning it essentially rolls along its orbit. This unusual tilt is thought to be the result of a colossal collision with an Earth-sized object long ago, causing extreme seasons lasting 21 Earth years each.

Uranus is an ice giant with an interior of hot, dense "icy" materials — water, methane, and ammonia — surrounding a small rocky core. Its blue-green colour comes from methane absorbing red light. Despite being farther from the Sun, Uranus is actually colder than Neptune, emitting very little internal heat.

Planet 08 · The Outermost

Neptune

Ice Giant · Windy Frontier World

Neptune is the eighth and most distant planet from the Sun. It is an ice giant with the strongest sustained winds of any planet — reaching speeds of up to 2,100 km/h. Its striking deep blue colour is enhanced by methane in the atmosphere and an unidentified absorber that makes it bluer than Uranus.

Neptune was the first planet predicted by mathematics before being directly observed. Its largest moon Triton has a retrograde orbit, suggesting it was captured from the Kuiper Belt. Triton, at –235 °C, is one of the coldest known objects in the Solar System and has nitrogen geysers erupting from its surface.

04 — Anatomy

Structure of the Solar System

The Solar System is not just planets — it is a complex set of regions, belts, clouds, and boundaries, each home to different types of objects.

☀️

The Sun

The gravitational and energy centre of the system. Contains 99.86% of all mass. Its core fuses 600 million tonnes of hydrogen per second, producing the energy that lights and warms the Solar System.

🪨

Inner Solar System

Contains the four rocky terrestrial planets (Mercury, Venus, Earth, Mars). These planets are small, dense, and have solid surfaces. The region extends out to about 2 AU from the Sun.

💫

Asteroid Belt

Located between Mars and Jupiter (2.2–3.2 AU), containing millions of rocky and metallic bodies — remnants of planetary formation prevented from coalescing by Jupiter's gravity. Ceres is the largest body here.

🌀

Outer Solar System

Home to the four giant planets — Jupiter, Saturn, Uranus, and Neptune. These massive worlds are made of gas and ices, with numerous moons and ring systems, extending from about 5 to 30 AU.

🧊

Kuiper Belt

A disc-shaped region beyond Neptune's orbit (30–50 AU) containing thousands of icy bodies. Pluto, Eris, and Makemake reside here. It is thought to be the source of short-period comets.

🌌

Scattered Disc

A sparsely populated region extending beyond the Kuiper Belt, containing icy objects with highly elliptical orbits. Eris resides here at up to 97.7 AU from the Sun.

☁️

Oort Cloud

A vast, spherical shell of icy bodies surrounding the Solar System at 2,000–100,000 AU. It is the source of long-period comets, and its outer boundary marks the practical edge of the Sun's gravitational influence.

🔵

Heliosphere

The vast bubble of solar wind and magnetic field generated by the Sun, extending beyond Neptune. The heliopause — where solar wind meets interstellar medium — was crossed by Voyager 1 in 2012.

05 — Minor Worlds

Dwarf Planets

Dwarf planets are round worlds that orbit the Sun but have not cleared the neighbourhood around their orbit. The IAU currently recognises five official dwarf planets.

Pluto

Once the 9th planet, reclassified in 2006. Located in the Kuiper Belt at ~39 AU. Has a heart-shaped nitrogen ice plain (Tombaugh Regio) and 5 moons, the largest being Charon.

Diameter: 2,377 km · Temp: –230 °C

Eris

The most massive known dwarf planet, in the scattered disc at up to 97.7 AU. Its discovery in 2005 was the main catalyst for Pluto's reclassification. Has one moon: Dysnomia.

Diameter: ~2,326 km · Temp: –243 °C

Ceres

The largest object in the asteroid belt and smallest recognised dwarf planet. NASA's Dawn spacecraft orbited it in 2015, revealing bright spots of salt deposits on its surface.

Diameter: 939 km · Located in Asteroid Belt

Makemake

A Kuiper Belt dwarf planet discovered in 2005. Covered in methane and nitrogen ices giving it a reddish-brown colour. Has one known moon: MK2.

Diameter: ~1,430 km · Temp: –239 °C

Haumea

One of the fastest rotating large objects in the Solar System — one rotation every four hours, stretching it into an egg-like shape. Has a ring and two moons: Hi'iaka and Namaka.

Length: ~1,960 km · 4-hr rotation

06 — Did You Know?

Cosmic Fun Facts

🌞 Sunlight Travel Time

Light from the Sun takes approximately 8 minutes and 20 seconds to reach Earth. It takes over 5 hours to reach Neptune, and more than 1.5 years to reach the outer edge of the Oort Cloud.

🪐 Saturn Would Float

Saturn has the lowest density of any planet — less than water. Theoretically, it would float if placed in an ocean large enough to hold it.

🌋 Io's Constant Eruptions

Jupiter's moon Io is the most volcanically active body in the Solar System, with over 400 active volcanoes continuously reshaping its surface. Plumes can reach 500 km high.

💧 Europa's Hidden Ocean

Jupiter's moon Europa likely has a subsurface ocean beneath its icy crust containing roughly twice the volume of all Earth's oceans — one of the most promising places to search for extraterrestrial life.

🔵 Neptune's Winds

Neptune has the most violent winds in the Solar System, with recorded speeds of up to 2,100 km/h — faster than the speed of sound on Earth.

🌑 Ganymede is Enormous

Jupiter's moon Ganymede is the largest moon in the Solar System — larger than the planet Mercury. It also has its own magnetic field, something no other moon possesses.

♨️ The Sun's Outer Layer

The Sun's corona (outer atmosphere) is paradoxically much hotter than the surface — reaching millions of degrees Celsius compared to 5,500 °C at the photosphere.

🏔️ Olympus Mons

Mars hosts the tallest volcano in the Solar System — Olympus Mons, standing 21 km high (2.5× Everest) and spanning 600 km in diameter, roughly the size of France.

🧲 Jupiter Protects Earth

Jupiter's enormous gravity acts as a cosmic vacuum cleaner, capturing or deflecting asteroids and comets that might otherwise collide with the inner planets.
